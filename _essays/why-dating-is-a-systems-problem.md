## Introduction

Most people treat dating as something that simply happens to them. They go on dates, feel things, react, and hope for the best. But the patterns that determine who you meet, who you choose, and how relationships develop are systematic — not random.

## Main Argument

When you look at dating through a systems lens, several things become clear. First, your selection criteria are often implicit rather than explicit. You think you know what you want, but your revealed preferences — the people you actually choose — tell a different story.

Second, the feedback loops in dating are long and noisy. You might not realise that a particular pattern is failing you until months or years have passed. By then, the signal is buried under emotional noise.

Third, most dating advice focuses on tactics rather than structure. It tells you what to say or how to behave, but it rarely addresses the underlying system that determines your outcomes.

## Implications

Treating dating as a systems problem means building better feedback loops, making your selection criteria explicit, and running structured experiments rather than relying on intuition alone. It means treating your dating life with the same rigour you would apply to any complex system you wanted to optimise.

## Conclusion

The shift from treating dating as a feelings problem to treating it as a systems problem is not about removing emotion. It is about building the structure that allows emotion to lead you somewhere meaningful rather than somewhere familiar.
