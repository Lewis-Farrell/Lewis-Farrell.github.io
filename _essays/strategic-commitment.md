## The Information Problem

Modern dating markets are plagued by information asymmetries. When two people meet, neither knows the other's true commitment intentions, relationship capacity, or long-term compatibility. Everyone claims to want something serious when it suits them. Words are cheap.

This creates a classic adverse selection problem. Without credible signals, high-commitment individuals cannot differentiate themselves from those simply looking for short-term engagement. The market becomes flooded with noise, and genuine matches become harder to identify.

## Costly Signals as Commitment Devices

Economic theory suggests that credible signals must be costly—specifically, more costly for those faking commitment than for those genuinely offering it. This cost differential is what makes the signal informative.

In dating contexts, these costly signals take many forms:

- **Time investment**: Consistently showing up over weeks and months, even when inconvenient
- **Opportunity cost**: Turning down other dating options to focus on one person
- **Vulnerability**: Sharing personal information that could be used against you
- **Resource allocation**: Planning elaborate dates, thoughtful gifts, or supportive actions

The key insight is that these investments are differentially costly. Someone genuinely interested in a long-term relationship with you finds these costs acceptable relative to the expected benefit. Someone pursuing short-term engagement finds them prohibitively expensive.

## Why Cheap Talk Fails

Simply stating commitment intentions is insufficient because it carries no cost for dishonest actors. There's no penalty for saying "I want something serious" while behaving otherwise. The signal contains no information.

This is why dating advice often emphasizes "watch what they do, not what they say." Actions that carry real cost—time, effort, vulnerability, foregone opportunities—are harder to fake and therefore more informative.

## The Screening Function

Costly signals serve a dual purpose. They not only reveal information about the sender but also actively screen potential partners. By requiring investment, you filter out those unwilling or unable to meet that standard.

This creates a natural sorting mechanism. Those who balk at reasonable investment requirements reveal themselves as poor matches for someone seeking commitment. Those who willingly bear these costs signal alignment with your relationship goals.

## Strategic Implications

Understanding costly signaling changes how you approach dating decisions:

1. **Be clear about your standards**: Requiring appropriate investment is not "playing games"—it's efficient screening
2. **Observe patterns over time**: Single actions are less informative than consistent behaviour
3. **Calibrate your own signals**: Ensure your investments match your stated intentions
4. **Don't punish honest signals**: If someone demonstrates costly commitment, treat that information seriously

The goal is not to make dating transactional but to acknowledge that in markets with imperfect information, credible signals require substance beyond words.

## Conclusion

Dating markets will always feature information asymmetries. The question is how to navigate them effectively. Costly signaling provides a framework for both sending and interpreting commitment signals in a world where cheap talk fails.

By understanding these dynamics, individuals can make better-informed decisions about who to invest in and how to demonstrate their own relationship intentions credibly.
